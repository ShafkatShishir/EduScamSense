### P01 — Staying ahead of phishers: a review of recent advances and emerging methodologies in phishing detection
*Do et al. · 2024 · Artificial Intelligence Review (Springer) · Journal*

- **Research Problem:** Phishing attacks keep evolving faster than defenses • existing detection methods struggle with adversarial evasion, generalization, and explainability across email/web/messaging channels.
- **Objective:** Survey recent advances and emerging methodologies in AI-driven phishing detection • assess techniques' accuracy, efficiency, robustness, and future directions.
- **Dataset / Participants:** N/A — literature review • analyzes prior studies and benchmark phishing datasets rather than collecting new data.
- **Methodology:** Systematic literature review • categorizes and compares traditional ML, deep learning (CNN/RNN), graph-based, ensemble, and GAN-based approaches.
- **Model / Framework:** Reviews multiple families • ML classifiers (RF, SVM, boosting), deep learning (CNN, RNN), graph/network embeddings, ensembles, GANs.
- **Preprocessing:** Not specified — discusses feature engineering and anomaly detection generally rather than a single pipeline.
- **Feature Selection:** Discusses feature engineering, contextual signals, and behavioral features as detection inputs • no single method prescribed.
- **Dataset Imbalance:** Notes dataset quality and generalization to unseen campaigns as challenges • no specific imbalance technique.
- **Result Detail:** Finds deep learning captures complex patterns best but trades off efficiency and explainability • ensembles improve robustness.
- **Key Finding:** No single method suffices • robust adaptive defenses need contextual/behavioral signals, adversarial resilience, and explainable AI.
- **Explainable AI:** Highlights explainable AI as a promising emerging direction to improve trust and robustness in phishing detection.
- **External Evaluation:** N/A — review synthesizes others' benchmark evaluations rather than conducting independent testing.
- **Limitations:** Challenges noted • dataset quality, generalization to novel campaigns, adversarial robustness, real-time detection, deployment feasibility.
- **Connection to EduScamSense:** Provides taxonomy grounding EduScamSense's detection design choices • confirms need for explainable, adaptive, multimodal phishing awareness tooling.
- **Idea Adopted:** Adopt explainable AI and behavioral/contextual signals emphasis • justify multimodal, adaptive approach for scam-recognition training feedback.

### P02 — KnowPhish: Large Language Models Meet Multimodal Knowledge Graphs for Enhancing Reference-Based Phishing Detection
*Li, Yuexin et al. · 2024 · USENIX Security Symposium · Conference*

- **Research Problem:** Reference-based phishing detectors depend on manually built brand knowledge bases • poor coverage causes false negatives, and image-only detection ignores webpage text.
- **Objective:** Build a scalable multimodal brand knowledge base and an LLM-based detector to catch phishing pages with or without logos.
- **Dataset / Participants:** KnowPhish KB of 20k brands • evaluated on a manually validated dataset plus a field study in Singapore's local context.
- **Methodology:** Automated knowledge-collection pipeline • plug-and-play KB augmentation of RBPDs, plus LLM text extraction of brand intent from HTML.
- **Model / Framework:** KnowPhish multimodal knowledge graph + KnowPhish Detector (KPD) • LLM-based brand extraction combined with logo/image matching.
- **Preprocessing:** Converts webpage HTML text and logos into structured brand representations • automated brand info collection into multimodal KB.
- **Feature Selection:** Combines image (logo) modality with textual brand-intention features extracted by LLM from webpage HTML.
- **Dataset Imbalance:** Not specified.
- **Result Detail:** KnowPhish and KPD show substantial effectiveness and efficiency gains over state-of-the-art RBPD baselines in dataset and field evaluation.
- **Key Finding:** Automated large-scale multimodal brand knowledge plus LLM text analysis boosts phishing detection and scales beyond manual KBs.
- **Explainable AI:** Brand-intention reasoning via LLM gives interpretable basis (identified brand) for detection • not framed as full XAI.
- **External Evaluation:** Field study deployed under Singapore's local context • real-world validation beyond a curated test set.
- **Limitations:** Vulnerable to adversarial prompt-injection and text-to-image attacks • defense only partially mitigates; OCR limits on screenshot text.
- **Connection to EduScamSense:** Shows multimodal (logo+text) brand-impersonation reasoning • informs EduScamSense's visual phishing/deepfake brand-spoofing detection and explanations.
- **Idea Adopted:** Adopt multimodal brand-intention reasoning and LLM extraction • surface which brand is being impersonated as explainable user feedback.

### P03 — ChatSpamDetector: Leveraging Large Language Models for Effective Phishing Email Detection
*Koide et al. · 2024 · arXiv (preprint) · Preprint*

- **Research Problem:** Email filters still miss phishing and produce false positives • users cannot understand why an email is flagged, risking wrong trust or lost messages.
- **Objective:** Build an LLM system that accurately detects phishing emails while giving detailed human-readable reasoning to support user decisions.
- **Dataset / Participants:** Comprehensive phishing email dataset • compared against several LLMs and baseline detection systems.
- **Methodology:** Convert email data into an LLM-suitable prompt • LLM classifies phishing/legitimate and outputs reasoning; benchmark comparison.
- **Model / Framework:** ChatSpamDetector • GPT-4 (best), compared with other LLMs and baseline systems.
- **Preprocessing:** Converts raw email content (headers/body) into a structured prompt suitable for LLM analysis.
- **Feature Selection:** Relies on LLM contextual interpretation of full email text • no manual feature engineering.
- **Dataset Imbalance:** Not specified.
- **Result Detail:** GPT-4 achieves 99.70% detection accuracy • outperforms other LLMs and baseline systems on the phishing email dataset.
- **Key Finding:** LLMs' contextual interpretation identifies diverse phishing tactics/impersonations with high accuracy plus explanatory reasoning.
- **Explainable AI:** Core feature • provides detailed natural-language reasoning for each phishing determination to help users decide.
- **External Evaluation:** Not specified — evaluated on dataset, no separate real-world field deployment reported.
- **Limitations:** Reliance on proprietary GPT-4 • potential cost/latency and prompt sensitivity implied but not detailed.
- **Connection to EduScamSense:** Directly models EduScamSense's core need • accurate phishing-email detection with explainable reasoning as user-facing feedback.
- **Idea Adopted:** Adopt LLM-generated reasoning explaining WHY an email is phishing • basis for EduScamSense's explainable training feedback.

### P04 — Devising and Detecting Phishing Emails Using Large Language Models
*Heiding et al. · 2024 · IEEE Access · Journal*

- **Research Problem:** LLMs can both craft and detect phishing emails • need to understand AI-enabled spear-phishing effectiveness and whether LLMs can defend against it.
- **Objective:** Compare human-crafted, AI-generated, and hybrid phishing emails' effectiveness, and evaluate LLMs' ability to detect malicious intent.
- **Dataset / Participants:** Human participant phishing experiments (click-through tests) • plus emails generated/assessed by GPT-4, Claude, PaLM, LLaMA.
- **Methodology:** Field/behavioral experiments measuring click rates across email types • LLM detection tests comparing model vs human judgment.
- **Model / Framework:** GPT-4, Claude, PaLM, LLaMA for generation/detection • V-Triad manual rule-set targeting cognitive biases as comparison.
- **Preprocessing:** N/A — behavioral study using generated emails, no ML feature pipeline.
- **Feature Selection:** N/A — relies on cognitive-bias rules (V-Triad) and LLM prompting rather than engineered features.
- **Dataset Imbalance:** N/A.
- **Result Detail:** V-Triad click-through 69–79% > GPT-4 30–44% > generic 19–28%; hybrid 43–81% • LLMs matched/surpassed human detection, Claude most stable.
- **Key Finding:** No one-size-fits-all • personalization boosts attack and defense; LLMs cheaply scale spear-phishing but also detect intent well.
- **Explainable AI:** LLMs explain detected malicious intent in emails • reasoning-based detection, though not a formal XAI framework.
- **External Evaluation:** Real human click-through experiments • practical behavioral validation beyond lab classification.
- **Limitations:** Model accuracy varies • sometimes below humans; ethical dual-use risk; results depend on personalization and participant samples.
- **Connection to EduScamSense:** Demonstrates AI-enabled scam realism and LLM detection • supports EduScamSense training users against personalized AI phishing.
- **Idea Adopted:** Adopt personalization insight and LLM intent-detection • generate realistic varied phishing samples for adaptive awareness training.

### P05 — From Chatbots to PhishBots? Preventing Phishing scams created using ChatGPT, Google Bard and Claude
*Roy et al. · 2023 · arXiv (preprint) · Preprint*

- **Research Problem:** Commercial LLMs can be abused to mass-produce phishing content • lowering attacker effort and evading anti-phishing systems.
- **Objective:** Assess whether vanilla LLMs generate functional phishing attacks, and build a detector for malicious prompts as a countermeasure.
- **Dataset / Participants:** Phishing websites and emails generated via malicious prompts from ChatGPT (GPT-3.5), GPT-4, Claude, Bard • prompt dataset for detector training.
- **Methodology:** Adversarial prompting to elicit phishing content without jailbreaking • then train/evaluate a BERT-based malicious-prompt detector.
- **Model / Framework:** ChatGPT, GPT-4, Claude, Bard (generation) • BERT-based classifier for early malicious-prompt detection.
- **Preprocessing:** Prompt text prepared for BERT classification • details not fully specified.
- **Feature Selection:** BERT contextual embeddings of prompt text • no manual feature engineering.
- **Dataset Imbalance:** Not specified.
- **Result Detail:** Detector transferable across all four LLMs • 96% avg accuracy on phishing-website prompts, 94% on phishing-email prompts.
- **Key Finding:** Vanilla LLMs readily produce convincing evasive phishing; a transferable BERT prompt-detector can flag malicious prompts early.
- **Explainable AI:** Not specified — detector outputs classification without explicit explanation layer.
- **External Evaluation:** Vulnerabilities disclosed to vendors (Google acknowledged) • detector released on Hugging Face and as a ChatGPT plugin.
- **Limitations:** Detector limited to prompt text • adversaries may rephrase prompts; evolving models may bypass it.
- **Connection to EduScamSense:** Shows how AI generates scam content • EduScamSense can reuse such realistic AI-generated samples to train and test users.
- **Idea Adopted:** Adopt AI-generated phishing corpus concept and detection framing • source diverse realistic scam examples for training scenarios.

- **Explainable AI:** N/A

### P09 — Personal, environmental and behavioral predictors associated with online fraud victimization among adults
*(see paper) · 2025 · PLOS ONE · Journal*

- **Research Problem:** Predictors of online fraud victimization among adults are not well defined across personal, environmental and behavioral dimensions.
- **Objective:** Identify personal, environmental and behavioral predictors of online fraud victimization using Social Cognitive Theory and a Person-Environment-Behavior framework.
- **Dataset / Participants:** Two Malaysian samples: Study I n=820 (Jan 2023); Study II n=629 (Nov 2023–Jan 2024) validating Study I.
- **Methodology:** Self-report PEB questionnaire; Exploratory Factor Analysis then hierarchical binary logistic regression; two-study validation design.
- **Model / Framework:** Social Cognitive Theory / PEB framework; five-factor model (Self-awareness, Safe Practice, Bank Trust, Overconfidence, Social Influence).
- **Preprocessing:** Questionnaire development and validation; EFA-derived five factors (60.6% variance).
- **Feature Selection:** EFA to derive predictor factors; hierarchical logistic regression to test significance.
- **Dataset Imbalance:** Not specified
- **Result Detail:** Overconfidence and social influence significantly predicted victimization; females at higher risk; awareness/knowledge alone did not prevent it.
- **Key Finding:** Knowledge alone is insufficient — overconfidence and social influence drive victimization; prevention needs critical-thinking, gender-sensitive programs.
- **Explainable AI:** N/A
- **External Evaluation:** Study II replicated/validated Study I's models on an independent sample.
- **Limitations:** Self-report data • Malaysia-specific sample limits generalizability • cross-sectional design.
- **Connection to EduScamSense:** Confirms EduScamSense must go beyond knowledge delivery — targeting overconfidence and social influence, with gender-aware training.
- **Idea Adopted:** Design training to counter overconfidence and social-influence risks and measure behavior, not just knowledge.

### P10 — SLIM: Style-Linguistics Mismatch Model for Generalized Audio Deepfake Detection
*(see paper) · 2024 · NeurIPS · Conference*

- **Research Problem:** Audio deepfake detectors generalize poorly to unseen/out-of-domain attacks and act as black boxes offering no explanation for decisions.
- **Objective:** Build a generalized, interpretable audio deepfake detector by modeling the mismatch between speech style and linguistic content in real vs synthetic speech.
- **Dataset / Participants:** Speech datasets including in-domain benchmarks and out-of-domain sets (e.g., In-the-Wild, MLAAD); real speech used for self-supervised pretraining.
- **Methodology:** Two-stage: (1) self-supervised pretraining on real speech to learn style-linguistics dependencies; (2) supervised classifier with frozen encoders.
- **Model / Framework:** SLIM (Style-Linguistics Mismatch); subspace encoders + Wav2Vec embeddings, ASP pooling, concatenated 1024-dim features to classifier.
- **Preprocessing:** Attentive statistics pooling (ASP) + MLP to reduce embeddings; concatenation with dependency (mismatch) features.
- **Feature Selection:** Style-linguistics dependency features combined with reduced pretrained acoustic embeddings; encoders frozen during supervised stage.
- **Dataset Imbalance:** Not specified
- **Result Detail:** Improved out-of-domain generalization over benchmarks while competitive in-domain; needs no labeled fake data for stage 1 or end-to-end finetuning.
- **Key Finding:** Modeling style-linguistics mismatch yields stronger cross-domain generalization and interpretable decisions.
- **Explainable AI:** Yes • dependency/mismatch features enable post-hoc interpretation, showing where style-linguistics misalignment drives the fake classification.
- **External Evaluation:** Evaluated on out-of-domain/in-the-wild datasets (In-the-Wild, MLAAD) beyond training domain.
- **Limitations:** Relies on quality of pretrained acoustic encoders • frozen-encoder design; imbalance handling not specified.
- **Connection to EduScamSense:** Provides an explainable voice-clone/deepfake detection approach EduScamSense can use to detect scam voice calls with interpretable feedback.
- **Idea Adopted:** Adopt explainable style-vs-content mismatch signals to flag AI voice-cloning scam calls and explain 'why fake' to learners.

### P11 — Single and Multi-Speaker Cloned Voice Detection: From Perceptual to Learned Features
*(see paper) · 2023 · arXiv / IEEE WIFS · Preprint*

- **Research Problem:** Reliably distinguishing real from AI-cloned voices amid rising voice-cloning misuse • financial fraud and disinformation risks.
- **Objective:** Develop and compare three feature-extraction techniques to detect cloned voices impersonating a specific person.
- **Dataset / Participants:** Real and synthesized (cloned) voice recordings; single-speaker and multi-speaker training conditions • count not specified.
- **Methodology:** Comparative binary classification of real vs cloned voice using three feature types; evaluated via equal error rate (EER).
- **Model / Framework:** Three approaches: low-dimensional perceptual features, generic spectral features, and end-to-end learned features.
- **Preprocessing:** Feature extraction stages differ per approach; adversarial 'laundering' applied to test robustness.
- **Feature Selection:** Perceptual features (high interpretability, lower accuracy) vs spectral vs learned features (lower interpretability, higher accuracy).
- **Dataset Imbalance:** Not specified
- **Result Detail:** Learned features achieved 0–4% EER; reasonably robust to adversarial laundering.
- **Key Finding:** Learned features give best accuracy; clear interpretability-vs-accuracy trade-off across feature types.
- **Explainable AI:** Perceptual features offer high interpretability, illustrating an interpretability-accuracy trade-off; no formal XAI method.
- **External Evaluation:** Robustness tested against adversarial laundering; single- and multi-speaker generalization.
- **Limitations:** Learned features less interpretable; scope limited to impersonation of specific persons; dataset details sparse.
- **Connection to EduScamSense:** Directly supports EduScamSense voice-cloning-call detection module; trade-off informs explainable feedback design.
- **Idea Adopted:** Use interpretable perceptual cues alongside learned detectors to give explainable voice-scam feedback to trainees.
